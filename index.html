1 Methods

Data preparation

Data were imported into R statistical software from the study Excel database and cleaned prior to analysis. Variable names were standardized and duplicate names were made unique. Binary responses recorded as “Yes/No”, “True/False”, or “1/0” were converted into numeric binary indicators using a custom conversion function. Missing responses were retained as missing values during recoding to avoid misclassification.

Age was categorized into 20–39, 40–49, 50–59, and ≥60 years. Education level was grouped into college/university, secondary, primary, and none. Marital status was categorized as married, never married, widowed, divorced/separated, or other. Years of service as a CHP were grouped into <5 years, 5–9 years, and ≥10 years.

Hypertension was defined as average systolic blood pressure ≥140 mmHg or average diastolic blood pressure ≥90 mmHg. Blood glucose values recorded in mmol/L were converted to mg/dL by multiplying by 18 where applicable, and diabetes was defined as fasting blood glucose ≥126 mg/dL. BMI was calculated as weight in kilograms divided by height in meters squared and categorized using standard WHO classifications.

Knowledge score construction

Separate knowledge scores were developed for hypertension and diabetes self-care management. Each score consisted of 10 questionnaire items assessing understanding of medication adherence, diet, physical activity, screening, risk factors, stress, and lifestyle modification. Reverse-coded items were corrected before score summation. For hypertension, items relating to medication use only when symptomatic and misconceptions regarding alcohol and smoking were reverse-coded. For diabetes, items relating to avoidance of fruits, misconceptions regarding alcohol and smoking, and misconceptions regarding fibre intake were reverse-coded.

Scores ranged from 0 to 10, with higher scores indicating greater knowledge. Knowledge scores were further categorized into low/moderate knowledge (<7) and high knowledge (≥7).

Self-care practice variables

Self-care practices were derived from multiple domains including medication adherence, dietary practices, physical activity, monitoring practices, risk behaviour control, healthcare utilization, and psychosocial support.

Medication adherence was categorized as good, moderate, or poor based on frequency of medication use. Dietary indicators included salt restriction, fruit and vegetable intake, adherence to recommended diet, avoidance of sugary or fatty foods, and portion control. Physical activity variables included walking, jogging, household chores, and total weekly moderate activity duration. Participants were classified as meeting WHO physical activity recommendations if they achieved at least 150 minutes of moderate activity per week.

Monitoring practices included frequency of blood pressure and glucose monitoring, ownership and functionality of blood pressure machines and glucometers, and a composite monitoring indicator. Risk behaviour variables included smoking, alcohol consumption, risky alcohol use, and stress management practices. Healthcare utilization indicators included clinic attendance, prompt care-seeking, and availability of medications. Psychosocial support variables included emotional support and self-efficacy.

Barriers and facilitators

Barrier variables included cost of treatment, time constraints, distance to facilities, lack of monitoring devices, inadequate knowledge, cultural beliefs or stigma, lack of training, and low prioritization of self-care. Facilitator variables included family support, peer support, access to devices, supervisor encouragement, training, free medicines, and mobile reminders.

Composite uptake variable

A composite self-care uptake variable was developed among participants with complete data on medication adherence, physical activity, and dietary adherence. Participants were classified as having uptake of self-care practices if they demonstrated good medication adherence, adherence to recommended dietary practices, and achievement of WHO physical activity recommendations simultaneously.

2 Statistical analysis

All analyses were conducted using R statistical software. Categorical variables were summarized using frequencies and percentages, while continuous variables were summarized using means and standard deviations.

Prevalence of hypertension and diabetes was estimated with 95% Wilson confidence intervals. Associations between participant characteristics and hypertension or diabetes status were assessed using chi-square or Fisher’s exact tests where appropriate.

Multivariable logistic regression models were used to identify factors associated with hypertension and diabetes. Results were presented as odds ratios (ORs) with 95% confidence intervals and p-values. Model diagnostics included assessment of goodness-of-fit using the Hosmer–Lemeshow test, model discrimination using the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC-ROC), model accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), and multicollinearity using variance inflation factors (VIFs).

Linear regression models were used to assess predictors of hypertension and diabetes knowledge scores, with results presented as beta coefficients and confidence intervals.

Cross-tabulations were conducted to assess relationships between knowledge categories and selected self-care practices. Additional exploratory analyses included Pearson correlation analysis, principal component analysis (PCA), and k-means clustering to identify patterns in knowledge, barriers, facilitators, and self-care practices among CHPs.

Tables, figures, forest plots, and analytical outputs were exported to Microsoft Word, Excel, CSV, and RDS formats to support reporting and reproducibility.

3.2 Top 3 Findings

  1. Hypertension burden was substantial among CHPs, affecting 36.4% of participants.

  2. Knowledge was very high, but self-care uptake was much lower, suggesting a clear knowledge–practice gap.

  3. Structural barriers mattered more than knowledge deficits, especially cost of testing/medicines, drug availability, access to devices, and workload constraints.

3.3 Main Implication

The findings suggest that CHP self-care interventions should go beyond refresher training. Programme responses should prioritize reducing medicine and testing costs, improving drug availability, expanding access to BP/glucose monitoring devices, and integrating CHP self-care into routine supervision.

11 Interpretation and Note

Interpretation of the findings

The study shows a clear knowledge–practice gap among CHPs. Knowledge of hypertension and diabetes self-care was very high, but actual self-care uptake was much lower and appeared to be shaped more by structural barriers than by knowledge alone.

Key findings

Among 601 CHPs, hypertension prevalence was 36.4%, while diabetes prevalence was 7.3%. This indicates a substantial burden of non-communicable diseases among CHPs themselves, especially hypertension.

Hypertension was strongly associated with age and obesity. Compared with CHPs aged 20–39 years, those aged 40–49, 50–59, and 60+ years had progressively higher odds of hypertension. Obese CHPs had about 2.5 times higher odds of hypertension compared with those with normal BMI. Male CHPs also had higher adjusted odds of hypertension.

Diabetes prevalence was lower, and the regression model showed no statistically significant independent predictors after adjustment. The diabetes model should be interpreted cautiously because there were only 44 diabetes cases, resulting in wide confidence intervals and limited statistical power.

Knowledge levels were very high: 99.6% had high hypertension knowledge and 97.2% had high diabetes knowledge. However, the low variability means knowledge score was not very useful for distinguishing between CHPs or predicting practices.

Among CHPs who self-reported hypertension, diabetes, or both, many reported good practices: 83.5% had good medication adherence, 91.8% met WHO physical activity recommendations, 99% monitored BP regularly, and 99% reported confidence in managing their condition. However, only 44.3% adhered to recommended diet, 35.1% reported drugs were always available, and cost/drug availability remained major concerns.

The most common barrier was cost of testing/medicines (60.8%), followed by lack of time/workload, distance to facility, and lack of devices. Key facilitators were family support (76.3%), access to devices, peer support, training, and supervisor encouragement.

Self-care uptake was defined strictly as having good medication adherence, meeting physical activity recommendations, and adhering to diet. Only 32 of 91 eligible CHPs met this composite uptake definition. In the uptake model, the cost barrier was significantly associated with uptake, but this finding needs careful interpretation because cost may identify CHPs actively engaged with care and therefore more aware of costs.

Overall interpretation

The main message is that CHPs know what should be done, but knowledge alone is not enough to ensure self-care uptake.

The very weak correlations between knowledge and barriers suggest that poor self-care is not mainly due to lack of knowledge. Instead, it is likely driven by cost, medicine availability, access to devices, service access, and workload constraints.

Programmatic implication

Interventions should not focus only on refresher training. They should prioritize: - reducing cost of medicines and testing; - ensuring reliable drug availability; - improving access to BP/glucose monitoring devices; - strengthening peer and family support; - integrating CHP self-care into routine supervision.

Bottom line

This is a strong and policy-relevant analysis. The strongest publishable finding is the contrast between high knowledge and incomplete self-care uptake, showing that structural and system-level barriers are more important than knowledge deficits.
